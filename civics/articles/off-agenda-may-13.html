What's Coming in Public Comment Wednesday — and Why the Council Can't Respond

The substantive issues many residents will speak to on May 13 — Esmeralda, the proposed water and sewer rate increases, a neighborhood's paving request — are not on Wednesday's agenda. Here's what's in the comment file and what state law lets the Council do about it.

May 8, 2026

The Brown Act — California's open-meetings law — bars city councils from substantively discussing or acting on any item not listed on the published agenda. So when residents stand at the mic Wednesday to address topics that aren't on the May 13 agenda, the Council can listen. They cannot, in the moment, debate, decide, or even meaningfully discuss.

That doesn't stop the comments from coming. Based on the public comment file submitted as of May 6, three topics are likely to come up.

The Esmeralda Development

The proposed Esmeralda development on the former Masonite property — a multi-phase residential and mixed-use project that has been working its way through the city's review process since November 2025 — is the topic generating the most written comment. The packet contains both opposition and support letters. The substantive concerns raised include:

CEQA process

Whether the 2009 Environmental Impact Report (with addenda from 2016, 2018, and 2026) is adequate for a project of this scale, given changes in the regulatory landscape since the original was certified.

Water supply and the Russian River

Letters from a retired environmental scientist with 45 years of riverine experience and from the Dry Creek Rancheria Band of Pomo Indians raise questions about cumulative impacts on river flows, threatened and endangered salmonid habitat (including coho, steelhead, and chinook salmon), and the well-documented hydraulic connection between groundwater pumping and surface-water flow in the Russian River system.

The Masonite site's contamination history

The site received a No Further Action determination from the North Coast Regional Water Quality Control Board in January 2026. A citizen petitioner has filed a request with the State Water Resources Control Board for a stay and review, citing concerns about a documented rebound in pentachlorophenol concentrations at one of the final monitoring wells before it was destroyed last December.

The case for the project

Letters in support note that Cloverdale's General Plan has anticipated growth on this scale for two decades and argue that the city's fiscal health depends on a mix of housing types beyond the low-income housing stock the city already has more of than any other Sonoma County city. Supporters also point to the project's planned hotel and commercial elements as sources of transient occupancy tax and property tax revenue.

The Esmeralda project itself is not on Wednesday's agenda. It will return on its own track.

The Proposed Water and Sewer Rate Increases

The Council took testimony on the five-year water and wastewater rate study at the April 22 meeting. The study, prepared by Hildebrand Consulting, lays out projected revenues, expenses, capital improvement needs, and long-term financial sustainability for both utilities. The proposed rates are now inside their Proposition 218 protest period — a process that allows ratepayers to formally object in writing.

Several formal protests are now in the public file, citing affordability concerns and the cumulative effect of recent increases. Since 2021, the base water rate, the flat sewer rate, and the water usage rate have each risen by roughly 50–68 percent. A separate citizen counter-proposal asks the city to slow the rate of increase, particularly for fixed-income households, and points to deferred infrastructure projects — including the Cherry Creek (Southcrest) reservoir replacement that was scheduled for 2022 — as evidence that ratepayers have been paying for capital work that hasn't yet been delivered.

Mayor Way Paving

A resident has formally requested inspection and prioritized repaving of Mayor Way, citing accelerated road damage from years of city bus traffic that was reportedly mis-routed through the residential street, standing water after rainfall, and pedestrian safety concerns for the neighborhood's older residents and children. The letter notes the recent approval of Measure DD funds and asks for a clear timeline.

What the Council Can Actually Do Wednesday

For any of the above, Wednesday's options are limited. Under the Brown Act, when an off-agenda item is raised in public comment, Councilmembers may:

  • Briefly respond to a statement or question
  • Ask staff for clarification or for a future report
  • Request that an item be placed on a future agenda (which requires Council consensus)

What they cannot do is deliberate, debate, or decide. That's not Council reluctance — it's state law, and it exists to make sure the public has notice before any government body acts.

If You Want to Be Heard

Verbal comment is in person only Wednesday — no Zoom mic. Three minutes per speaker. Written comment can be sent to publiccomments@ci.cloverdale.ca.us and is read into the public record. Submissions are public records under the California Public Records Act, so anything you send is disclosable.

A reminder for everyone planning to comment: keep it civil and keep it focused. The Council can hear you. They cannot, on Wednesday, debate you. The most effective comments are usually the ones that ask for something specific the Council can actually put on a future agenda.
