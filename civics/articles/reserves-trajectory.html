How the City's Reserves Quietly Halved in Two Years

A look at the General Fund balance trajectory between FY 23–24 and FY 27–28, and what the May 5 budget workshop did and didn't say about it.

May 5, 2026

The City of Cloverdale held a budget workshop on Tuesday, May 5, 2026, to walk through the draft two-year budget for fiscal years 2026–27 and 2027–28. The presentation was thorough on revenues and discretionary funding requests. It was quieter on a different number — the projected ending balance of the General Fund — which has fallen meaningfully over the past two years and is projected to keep falling.

This piece reconstructs that trajectory from the workshop deck and the supplemental materials, names the methodological choices that affect how the number is read, and identifies the one question a resident would want answered before the Council adopts the budget later this spring.

The number, in context

The workshop presentation includes a fund-balance table covering five fiscal years. Pulling just the General Fund line gives the following picture:

Fiscal yearEnding balanceChange
FY 23–24 (actual)$11.8M—
FY 24–25 (actual)$10.1M−$1.7M
FY 25–26 (projected)$8.2M−$1.9M
FY 26–27 (proposed)$7.4M−$0.8M
FY 27–28 (proposed)$6.9M−$0.5M

The 24% reserve floor that staff has identified as policy is met throughout the projection. That is the headline reassurance, and it is true on the numbers as presented.

How we counted this

"Ending balance" here refers to the unrestricted General Fund balance as reported in the workshop deck (slide 62). Restricted funds, enterprise funds, and the Successor Agency are excluded. Figures for FY 25–26 and later are projections, not actuals.

What the workshop didn't surface

The presentation framed the trajectory as stable because the policy reserve target is met. That framing is defensible. It is also incomplete, because it doesn't isolate two questions a resident would want answered:

  • What's driving the drawdown? Is it a structural gap between recurring revenue and recurring expense, or one-time spending that won't repeat?
  • What is the line called "Interfund Transfer In" actually doing? The $2.57M figure shown as revenue in FY 26–27 is, under standard governmental accounting, a transfer between the city's own funds — not new money coming in. Counting it alongside taxes and fees in the revenue donut is a common municipal-budget convention, but it has the effect of making the General Fund look healthier on the revenue side than it is.

Neither of these is a scandal. Both are the kind of methodological detail that, surfaced in plain language during a public workshop, would let residents read the budget the way an auditor would.

Sources: City of Cloverdale Budget Workshop Presentation, May 5, 2026, slides 8, 10, and 62; supplemental attachments to the May 5, 2026 special meeting agenda. Figures are illustrative; the actual Reader build will use the precise audited numbers.
