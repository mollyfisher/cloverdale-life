Exhibit A · Self-initiated patrol activity

Cloverdale leads the region in officer-initiated calls.

Share of all activity that officers begin themselves — security checks, building checks, on-view stops — rather than responding to a citizen call.

Sebastopol publishes monthly counts (June through October 2025 averaged 54% officer-initiated). Cloverdale runs 61% — its security-check tempo is unusually heavy. Of those 1,938 officer-initiated incidents, the overwhelming majority resolved as "Checks Ok" or "Completed."
Exhibit B · Workload per sworn officer

Cloverdale officers carry the lightest per-head load.

Total annualized incidents divided by sworn patrol officers and 52 weeks.

Per officer, Cloverdale handles roughly half a Sebastopol officer's weekly volume — and a third less than Healdsburg, which Chief Jenkins has publicly described as operating with chronic vacancies. Adding a fourteenth officer in Cloverdale would lower an already low ratio.

The Verdict, Reluctantly Rendered

More cops or more cameras?
Not on this data.

Seventeen weeks of complete records, three peer-city comparisons, two staffing benchmarks, and one inescapable conclusion: the case for either expansion fails on the merits. Cloverdale's department generates so much idle-time activity that 61% of everything it logs is something it found for itself to do. Per officer, the workload is the lightest of any nearby city. Per resident, the arrest rate is one-third Sebastopol's. The crimes that dominate Flock's marketing materials — auto theft, organized burglary crews, stolen-plate hits — appear in the logs at rates of two, three, and three across four months.

None of which means the department isn't doing its job. The Reveille files going back to 1973 show the same pattern: a quiet town that calls when the dog won't stop barking, when the Yukon hasn't moved in two weeks, and occasionally when something genuinely dangerous happens. The DUI hydrant crash on May 1 was handled. The eBike thief on May 2 got chased into a vineyard. The disturbance arrest on May 1 produced one Elbridge Avenue father-and-son cool-down. Thirteen officers handled all of it.

The honest argument for adding a fourteenth officer or one more Flock camera would have to rest on something other than the numbers in the logs — projected growth, geographic coverage of the unincorporated edge, mental-health response capacity, or specific service gaps. Those arguments deserve to be made on their own terms, not under cover of a vague "rising crime" sense the data simply does not support. Sebastopol's path of asking hard questions before expanding surveillance, and Healdsburg's path of agendizing a Flock review, are both more defensible than the unexamined drift toward more.
