Exhibit A · Self-initiated patrol activity

Cloverdale leads the region in officer-initiated calls.

Share of all activity that officers begin themselves — security checks, building checks, on-view stops — rather than responding to a citizen call.

Sebastopol publishes monthly counts (Jun–Oct 2025 averaged 54%). Cloverdale runs 57% across the full year — the security-check tempo is heavy and consistent. Of the 5,449 officer-initiated incidents, the overwhelming majority resolved as "Checks Ok" or "Completed."
Exhibit B · Workload per sworn officer

Cloverdale officers carry the lightest per-head load.

Total annualized incidents divided by sworn patrol officers and 52 weeks.

Per officer, Cloverdale handles roughly half a Sebastopol officer's weekly volume — and a quarter less than Healdsburg, which Chief Jenkins has publicly described as operating with chronic vacancies. Adding a fourteenth officer in Cloverdale would lower an already low ratio.

Exhibit D · The 24-hour clock

The 8 PM mountain is the park-closing routine.

Distribution of all 9,479 incidents across the hours of the day. Highlighted bars are the patrol-driven peaks.

The towering 8 PM bar — 1,101 incidents across the year — is the routine park-closing checks at Elbridge, West Second, and Healdsburg Avenue. The 7 AM spike is the same checks in reverse: opening rounds. The 11 AM bump is downtown patrol. These three rituals account for nearly a third of all annual activity.

The Verdict, Reluctantly Rendered

More cops or more cameras?
Not on this data.

Forty-seven weeks of complete records, three peer-city comparisons, two staffing benchmarks, and one inescapable conclusion: the case for either expansion fails on the merits. Cloverdale's department generates so much idle-time activity that 57% of everything it logs is something it found for itself to do. Per officer, the workload is the lightest of any nearby city. Per resident, the arrest rate is roughly a quarter of Sebastopol's. The crimes that dominate Flock's marketing materials — auto theft, organized burglary crews, stolen-plate hits — appear in the logs at rates of ten, nine, and twenty-five across an entire year.

None of which means the department isn't doing its job. The Reveille files going back to 1973 show the same pattern: a quiet town that calls when the dog won't stop barking, when the abandoned RV hasn't moved in two weeks, and occasionally when something genuinely dangerous happens. The Hit and Run Felony at the Quik Stop on May 24 was handled — vehicle vs. motorcycle trike, suspect arrested. The kidnapping on Chablis Way in July resolved within the day. The eBike thieves of summer got chased into vineyards. The Friday Night Live evenings produced exactly the parking-violation surge a small department manages with cones and overtime. Thirteen officers handled all of it.

The honest argument for adding a fourteenth officer or a fifteenth Flock camera would have to rest on something other than the numbers in the logs — projected growth, geographic coverage of the unincorporated edge, mental-health response capacity, or specific service gaps. Those arguments deserve to be made on their own terms, not under cover of a vague "rising crime" sense the data simply does not support. Sebastopol's path of asking hard questions before expanding surveillance, and Healdsburg's path of agendizing a Flock review, are both more defensible than the unexamined drift toward more.
