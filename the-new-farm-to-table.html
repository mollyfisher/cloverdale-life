~ what the climate actually grows ~

No matter what happens to the dams, we know that water is going to be more expensive.

For a hundred and seventeen years, water has been diverted from the Eel River into Potter Valley through the Potter Valley Project to grow crops that don't belong in a Mediterranean climate. That arrangement is ending — Scott Dam and Cape Horn Dam are slated for removal, and the diversion that defined Mendocino County agriculture for over a century is being restructured.

What grows in Potter Valley without imported summer water is not a hardship. It is a cuisine. It is what the soil, the rainfall, and the dry summer were built for. We know our bodies were built for the Mediterranean diet, too.

The vines that don't need the diversion

Old vines, heritage wines

Some of Potter Valley's old head-pruned vineyards were planted before Scott Dam was built in 1922. Their roots run fifteen feet down into alluvium that recharges with winter rain. They have never been irrigated. They survive every drought, including 2012-2016 and 2020-2022. These dry-farmed heritage vineyards make the most distinctive wines in Mendocino County, and they are the part of California viticulture least exposed to what is coming.

Zinfandel
The classic California heritage variety. Old head-trained Zin from Mendocino is sought by Bedrock, Carlisle, Turley, Ridge.
Carignan
Drought-tough, deep-rooted, and finally getting the respect it deserves. Old-vine Carignan defines several Mendocino bottlings.
Petite Sirah
Often co-planted with Zin in the old field-blend blocks. Inky, structured, ages beautifully.
Mataro / Mourvèdre
A heat-loving Mediterranean grape that handles dry summers without irrigation. Pairs with the land.
Trousseau & Valdiguié
Lighter-bodied old-California varieties enjoying a renaissance with sommeliers and natural-wine drinkers.
Mixed Black Field Blends
The historic Italian-immigrant pattern: many varieties co-planted, picked together. The wines tell the planting's story.

Five principles for eating with the valley

i.

Perennials over annuals.

Deep-rooted plants drink winter rain stored in the soil profile. Shallow annuals demand summer irrigation that won't be reliably available.

ii.

Mediterranean palates, not Midwestern ones.

Olive oil, lamb, beans, grain, stone fruit, wine. The Italian and Greek countryside ate from this climate for millennia. So can we.

iii.

Seasonal abundance, stored well.

A dry-summer climate feeds people year-round only through preservation. Drying, milling, curing, fermentation, oil-pressing — the preservation arts are what turn a wet-winter, dry-summer system into reliable food.

iv.

Local infrastructure or it doesn't happen.

A mill, a press, a slaughterhouse, a creamery, a winery for small lots. The bottleneck isn't what grows — it's who buys, processes, and distributes. Build that, or none of this works.

v.

Match what's grown to what falls from the sky.

A crop that requires more summer water than the climate provides is a crop that depends on someone else's river. Plant for thirty inches of winter rain and a dry July, and the system works without taking from anywhere else.

What we need to build

The crops in this document are the easy part. The bottleneck for Mendocino County agriculture is processing, aggregation, and finance. Here is what a working post-diversion food economy actually requires — concrete pieces of local food infrastructure, mostly buildable with a few million dollars and a few years of patient cooperative work.

01

Community oil press

Olive oil must be pressed within 24 to 48 hours of harvest, so the press has to be close. The same equipment, with different fittings, handles sunflower, safflower, and walnut. One investment, many uses. McEvoy in Marin demonstrates the model at one scale; a Potter Valley or Ukiah Valley shared press would serve a hundred small growers.

Capital: moderate · Coop-friendly
02

Small-lot custom crush

A facility pitched at the heritage-vineyard scale, where a grower with five acres of old-vine Carignan can produce a few hundred cases under their own label without building a full winery. This is the missing link that lets old vines transition from depressed bulk-grape prices to premium bottled-wine prices.

Capital: moderate · Existing partial models
03

Regional slaughterhouse or mobile harvest unit

The single biggest infrastructure gap in Northern California small-scale meat. USDA-inspected facilities have consolidated to almost nothing. Mendocino's grass-fed beef and lamb economy depends on solving this. A regional facility — or a mobile unit serving the county — changes the math for every dryland grazing operation.

Capital: significant · Highest leverage
04

Grain mill and seed cleaning

Without a working mill, heritage grain has no path to market. A small-scale stone mill, a roller mill for finer flour, plus cleaning and storage equipment for whole grain. The infrastructure isn't large or expensive in isolation, but doesn't pencil for any single farm. The lesson of the Mendocino Grain Project is that it has to be cooperatively owned to survive.

Capital: modest · Coop required
05

Aggregation and cold storage

A working aggregation point where multiple small farms pool produce, store briefly, and ship together to Bay Area restaurants and grocers. Without it, every farmer is their own distributor, which is the time-suck that breaks small-farm economics. Mendo Lake Food Hub demonstrates the model partially; it needs to scale.

Capital: modest · Operations-heavy
06

Shared commercial kitchen

A licensed kitchen where a farmer turns walnuts into walnut butter, stone fruit into preserves, tomatoes into sauce. The capital cost is moderate; the regulatory navigation is the hard part. Once it exists, every farm in the region can diversify into value-added products without each one building their own facility.

Capital: modest · Regulatory-heavy
07

Bean cleaning and packaging

Smaller than grain but real. A regional small-scale bean cleaner-grader-packager lets local growers sell heirloom beans without depending on Napa intermediaries. The equipment is modest; the market exists.

Capital: small · Quick to start
08

Regional brand and appellation work

A Potter Valley olive oil designation. A heritage-vineyard wine certification. A regional grass-fed meat brand. These are paperwork and coordination, not capital projects, but they are real infrastructure for the price premiums that justify the production costs.

Capital: minimal · Coordination-heavy
09

Patient capital and cooperative governance

Most of this infrastructure pays back over ten to twenty years, not three to five. Conventional small-business loans don't fit. California FarmLink, RSF Social Finance, Kitchen Table Advisors, Slow Money networks, and tribal economic development funds are the right capital sources. Farmer-owned cooperatives are the right ownership structure.

Capital: enabling · Foundational
10

Salmon and watershed infrastructure

Looking ahead: smokehouses, processing for catch, freezer space for tribal subsistence harvests and eventual commercial fisheries. A longer arc, but worth naming. The Yurok Tribe has been building this on the Klamath. The Round Valley Tribes and the Mendocino Pomoan bands will need their own as the Eel recovers.

Capital: variable · Tribal-led

None of these projects require federal intervention or a major political fight. Most could be built around existing partial infrastructure — Mendo Lake Food Hub, the regional custom crush operations, the slaughter networks already trying to expand.

The Round Valley Indian Tribes and the Hopland Band are potential partners. Tribal economic development funding is real. The political alignment around the watershed creates conditions where tribal–agricultural partnerships are more available than they were five years ago.

The hopeful piece of this whole conversation is that the answer is "build these specific things" rather than "fight these specific people."
